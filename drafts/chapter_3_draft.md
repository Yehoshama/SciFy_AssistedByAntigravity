The silence of the *Zenith* was absolute as we prepared for our final jump. It was a silence born not of awe, but of exhaustion. 

Two and a half years of staring into the void, and we were exactly where we had started. We had scanned four sectors. Thousands of stars, tenths of Thousands of planets, all spinning in the terrifying darkness, completely devoid of anything that mattered. The grand, rational expedition that had launched from Protos with so much pomp and pride was ending with a whimper.

I sat at my primary Intelligence console on the bridge. My screens flickered with the green glow of tactical data, displaying the familiar, empty star charts of our assigned jump coordinates. The *Zenith* was returning to known space after one final, obligatory sweep.

Captain Aris Voss stood near the center of the bridge, his posture rigid. If he felt the crushing weight of the mission's failure, he didn't show it. His secondary training in psychology was fully engaged, his face an impenetrable mask of composed authority, calculating the optimal tone and body language to keep the skeleton crew functioning for the next forty-eight hours until we hit Protos space.

"All departments, report status for jump prep," Voss commanded, his voice cutting through the heavy silence of the bridge.

"Engineering is green," came the voice of Chief Engineer Elara over the comms. She sounded sharp, perhaps a little too fast, betraying the underlying stress. "The drive coils are spooled. We are ready to break local space, Captain. Medical bays are locked down per protocol, just in case." As her secondary role was medical, Elara was always the one to confirm the collision-dampeners were active in the med bays before a jump.

"Xenobiology is secured," Lyra's voice followed, sounding hollow compared to the bright, enthusiastic tone she had carried two years ago. "All samples stored. We're ready to go home."

"Intelligence is green," I said, my voice blending into the chorus of routine. "Sensors are nominal. No tactical threats detected."

"Navigation is green," Kael added from the pilot’s station. "The jump path is calculated and the corridor is clear."

Voss nodded. "Initiate jump sequence on my mark. Three. Two. One. Mark."

The familiar, low-frequency hum of the drive began to vibrate through the deck plates. It was a sound we all knew well—the sound of physics bending to human will. The hum grew into a roar, shaking the massive, self-sustaining city of the *Zenith*. Outside the viewport, the stars began to stretch into long, needle-thin lines of blinding white light as we tore a hole into the fabric of space.

Then, the sound changed.

It wasn't a sudden explosion, but a sickening, high-pitched whine that cut right through my skull. My console screamed warnings in a dozen different frequencies. The stable green glow of my tactical displays violently shifted to a bloody, flashing red. 

"Warning! Anomaly in spatial translation!"

The ship shuddered violently, a massive jolt that threw me hard against my restraints. The artificial gravity failed for half a second, sending loose datapads spinning through the air before slamming back down with bone-jarring force. The lights flickered, strobed, and died, replaced instantly by the eerie, crimson glare of emergency power.

Through the comms, Elara sounded panicked. "Captain! The containment field on the starboard drive coil is fluctuating! We have a cascade failure in the jump matrix! Initiating emergency drop!"

"Do it!" Voss shouted, gripping his console as another violent shudder tore through the ship. 

The roar of the drive abruptly cut off, replaced by the terrifying sound of shearing metal echoing through the bulkheads. We were thrown forward as the *Zenith* slammed out of the jump stream, tumbling wildly. The ship groaned like a dying animal before the dampeners finally caught us, stabilizing our momentum. 

Silence slammed back down on the bridge. It was different this time. It wasn't the silence of boredom. It was the silence of terror.

"Damage report, Elara," Voss commanded immediately, his voice completely level despite the alarms wailing around us. "Talk to me."

"The drive is fried, Captain," Elara gasped through the comms, the sound of sparking conduits in the background. "The matrix shattered. Main power is failing across multiple decks and requires immediate repairs to prevent a core breach! We are absolutely crippled."

"Divert auxiliary power to critical systems and containment," Voss said sharply, re-establishing control. "Vance, what's our position? Calculate our drift and re-orient the sensors."

I was already frantically typing on my console, trying to parse the scrambled telemetry. "Primary scanners are completely down," I said, my heart roaring in my ears. "LIDAR, thermal, standard telemetry—all offline. The ship has too many malfunctions and electromagnetic interference is too high. I can only get optics and visuals online." 

"Put it on the primary viewport," Voss ordered as the blast shields slowly retracted.

I routed the visual feed, expecting to see the familiar constellations of the Protos border sectors, or even the faint glow of the orbital rings. 

Instead, the screen refreshed, filling the viewport with massive, jagged silhouettes slowly tumbling through the dark. Asteroids. Thousands of them, larger than cities, drifting dangerously close to the crippled *Zenith*. 

Beyond the floating rocks, the void was pierced by the harsh light of an unknown sun. And beyond that local star, the sky was filled with starlight—but none of it was right. The familiar, glowing bands of the Helios Cluster were gone. Every constellation, every distant galaxy we had ever mapped, had been entirely replaced by an ocean of completely foreign stars. 

"I'm running visual star chart recognition," I said, my hands trembling as I forced the computer to cross-reference the alien star field against every mapped sector humanity had ever recorded. The search algorithm finished in seconds. 

Zero matches.

"Captain," I whispered, the hyper-rational foundation of my entire life cracking in a single terrible instant. "I don't know where we are. There is no match for this star field. We are in an unmapped asteroid cluster."

We were adrift in unknown space, trapped among the rocks in a dying ship. And for the first time in my life, logic had no answer.
