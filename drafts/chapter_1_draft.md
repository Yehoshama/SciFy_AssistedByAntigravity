The sky above Protos was a mathematically perfect shade of azure, unbroken by smog, industry, or the chaotic whims of unmanaged weather systems. All of that—the grinding, belching machinery required to sustain a civilization of fifty billion souls—hung in a neat, silver halo in low orbit. Down here, the world was a garden. 

I stood in the courtyard of the Academy of Sciences and Strategy, adjusting the collar of my new uniform. The fabric was stiff, smelling faintly of static and ozone. It was the uniform of an Intelligence Officer. 

"You're fidgeting, Vance."

I didn't need to turn around to know it was Lyra. She stepped up beside me, her own uniform bearing the deep green trim of the Xenobiology department. We were both silent for a moment, looking up at the orbital rings that gleamed like a razor blade cutting across the perfect blue sky.

"It’s the collar," I said, dropping my hand. "It doesn't breathe. It’s designed to look imposing, not function optimally."

Lyra offered a thin, tired smile. "Form over function? Careful, Kaelen. That sounds dangerously close to sentiment."

I snorted. We had survived four years of the most grueling physical and mental conditioning humanity had ever devised, solely to root out sentiment. You didn't become an Intelligence Officer—and you certainly didn't earn a secondary certification in Xenobiology—by letting emotion cloud your analytics. The curriculum was designed to push us to the absolute brink of systemic failure. They tested our lung capacities under low oxygen, tested our moral calculus in zero-sum game theories, and forced us to digest centuries of evolutionary biology until our brains felt like overtaxed processors. 

They did this to ensure we had no breaking point. A system with a breaking point was a poorly designed system. Protos did not tolerate poor design.

"Are you ready?" she asked, her voice dropping a fraction of a decibel. 

"I have run the projections," I replied automatically. "I am prepared for my primary duties, and I have allocated optimal downtime to assist in your lab."

"You know what I mean, Kaelen." 

She was looking at the massive transport ship slowly descending toward the spaceport on the horizon. It was a shuttle, but compared to anything else in the sky, it was a behemoth. It would take us to the *Zenith*, waiting silently in orbit. 

To the citizens of Protos, we were heroes. It was an unspoken, highly irrational reverence. We had cured every disease and mapped every genetic variance, entirely discarding the archaic, shameful concepts of gods or creators. But beneath the cold logic, there was a quiet, almost desperate eagerness—a collective societal longing to know that we were not alone. The explorers who went into the dark were revered because they were sent to answer the final variable in our algorithm: the search for another voice in the silence.

As we gathered in the massive graduation amphitheater, the atmosphere crackled with that suppressed excitement. Thousands of newly minted officers and crew members stood at perfect attention. The High Chancellor took the podium, his voice projected with perfect clarity.

"Today, you leave the garden," the Chancellor said, his gaze sweeping over the rows of pristine uniforms. "You are the vanguard of our logic, the absolute best minds of fifty billion citizens. You sail on the *Zenith* not just to map dead rocks, but to find us a neighbor. Bring back proof that our civilization is not an anomaly." 

The resulting cheer from the newly minted explorers broke all protocol—a thunderous, uncalculated wave of pure, hopeful excitement that reverberated in my chest. Even Lyra was smiling, caught up in the brilliant promise of it. 

As we walked out of the hall, the reality of our strict society immediately reasserted itself. We passed a group of first-year cadets, and I overheard an exchange that made my jaw tighten. An instructor was dressing down a cadet who had brought up a piece of pre-Unification literature—a text referencing a 'divine architect.'

"It is a pathetic crutch," the instructor was saying, his voice perfectly even, devoid of anger but dripping with clinical pity. "A failure of primitive minds to comprehend the complexity of quantum mechanics and biological evolution. To assign a 'Creator' to a process you simply do not yet understand is the ultimate intellectual cowardice. Do you understand, Cadet?"

"Yes, Sir. I apologize for the inefficiency of my question."

Lyra caught my eye as we passed them. It was a stark reminder. We desperately wanted to find civilizations out there, but we were fully equipped with the absolute certainty that there was no god, no creator, and nothing out there *greater* than ourselves.

I looked back up at the sky, tracing the line of the orbital rings. For a moment, the rigidity of my new collar didn't bother me. I was ready to leave the garden.
