The conference room was located just aft of the bridge, a sleek, windowless oval of polished chrome and frosted glass. Usually, the lighting in here was adjusted to mimic the warm, golden afternoon of Protos. Today, the emergency backups cast everything in a harsh, unforgiving white. 

I took my seat at the far end of the table. Usually, I sat two seats down, near Lyra and the science division. Today, I was representing the ship's entire defensive capability as Senior Military Officer. The fact that the seat was finally being used for its primary intention was terrifying.

Captain Aris Voss stood at the head of the table. His uniform was immaculate, a stark contrast to the soot and oil staining the rest of us. It was a calculated psychological anchor. If the captain appeared perfectly composed, the crew's logic would follow. 

"We are blind, dead in the water, and missing critical data," Voss began, his voice devoid of any inflection. It was the tone of a mathematician observing an uncomfortable variable. "I need facts. Extrapolate where necessary, but label your assumptions. Elara, start."

Chief Engineer Elara didn't stand. She leaned heavily on the table, wiping a smear of conductive grease from her cheek. "The main chemical maneuvering thrusters are nearly dry. The primary ion engines are completely offline due to cascade failure in the plasma regulators." She tapped a datapad, projecting a wireframe of the *Zenith* onto the center console. The entire aft section pulsed red. "However, the ion engines are repairable. If we cannibalize non-essential systems like the hydro-gardens' secondary filtration, I can bring main propulsion back online."

"Time frame?" Voss asked. 

"Fourteen days," Elara said grimly. "And that's optimistic. But... Captain. The jump drive is gone."

The silence in the room thickened. 

"Define 'gone'," Voss ordered. 

"The entire spatial-translation matrix is slag, along with the focusing rings," she replied, her voice tightening. "It cannot be repaired. A new one must be fabricated from scratch using the ship's heavy manufacturing bays. And Captain... we are talking about the most complex piece of technology humanity has ever engineered. Even if we had all the raw materials sitting in our cargo hold today, precision manufacturing, calibrating the quantum variances, and safely testing a new jump drive from scratch... it will take years. Decades, maybe."

Voss absorbed the information without blinking. He turned his gaze to Dr. Varis, the Chief Medical Officer, a gray-haired man whose secondary specialty in trauma surgery had just been brutally tested. 

"Casualties, Varis?"

Varis looked down at his own datapad, though he clearly already knew the numbers by heart. "Currently, fifteen percent of the crew is incapacitated with varying levels of severity. Mostly blunt-force trauma from the artificial gravity failure, and severe burns from electrical fires on the lower decks." He paused, clearing his throat. "We have thirty-seven confirmed dead."

The number hung in the air. Thirty-seven lives extinguished in a matter of hours. On Protos, a single accidental death would result in months of systemic review. Here, it was a sudden, violent statistic. 

"The majority of the deceased were the crews of the utility mining shuttles," Varis added quietly. "Their sacrifice bought the fighters the time they needed."

Voss nodded once, acknowledging the data point before moving to the next variable. He looked at Quartermaster Silas, the Head of Logistics, and Dr. Soren, the Chief Science Officer. 

"We need a new jump drive," Voss said. "Silas, do we have the materials in reserve?"

Quartermaster Silas shook his head, his wide frame shifting uncomfortably in his seat. "No, Captain. The ship was provisioned for maintenance, not absolute replacement of a tier-one component. I can manufacture the hull plating and basic circuitry, but we lack the rare-earth elements required for the jump drive's focusing rings—specifically, refined iridium and raw palladium."

"Which brings us to the rocks outside," Dr. Soren interjected, tapping his long fingers against the table. Soren was an astrophysicist primarily, but without functioning long-range sensors, his entire department was relying on short-range LIDAR pinged from the fighters. "Captain, I am operating on massive assumptions here. But based on the optical spectrometry Vance's team gathered during the attack, this asteroid field is incredibly dense and resource-rich. If we can secure a stable orbit, we can mine the exact elements Silas needs."

Voss fell silent for a long moment, processing the data. The equation was simple but brutal. If they stayed, they risked being crushed. If they couldn't mine, they would die of old age before ever going home. 

The Captain’s gaze finally landed on me. "Vance. Military status."

I sat up straighter, projecting the tactical map onto the table alongside Elara's diagnostic. "The fighter squadron is currently holding a spherical perimeter at thirty kilometers, acting as our eyes and our shield. They are shooting down or deflecting any debris entering the kinetic zone. But they cannot fly indefinitely. The pilots are exhausted."

"Your recommendation?" Voss asked.

"I need every crew member with a primary or secondary military designation pulled from their civilian duties immediately," I stated, the cold logic of the Academy overriding my own exhaustion. "We must mobilize them permanently. Half will rotate into the fighter squadron to relieve the pilots. The other half will act as fast-response disaster teams inside the ship to assist Engineering and Medical. We cannot rely on standard operational protocols."

Voss stared at the map. The scattered red dots of the asteroids vastly outnumbered the tiny green blips of my fighters. 

"Agreed," Voss said, standing up. "Mobilize the military reserve. Silas, coordinate with Soren's science teams to identify the most resource-dense asteroids within safe towing distance. Elara, prioritize the main engines, sensors, and structural integrity. The jump drive is officially sidelined. Short and medium-term survivability is our only priority now; a generational repair project for a new jump drive is irrelevant if we die tomorrow. We are sitting in the densest part of this cluster, and I will not wait here to be crushed."

He looked around the room, meeting each of our eyes. "We will use the last of the chemical thrusters to push the ship out of the immediate danger zone and find a sparser, stable clearing. Once there, we anchor. Then, we rebuild everything else."

***

The next forty-eight hours were a blur of methodical, desperate labor. The *Zenith* transformed from an exploration vessel into an industrial survival platform. 

Everywhere I went, the newly mobilized military response teams were in motion, their tactical armor a stark contrast to the sterile white corridors. They pried open jammed bulkheads with thermal cutters, assisted medical personnel in lifting crushed girders off injured crewmates, and stood ready at the flight-deck launch tubes. 

The ship hummed with an ugly, visceral vibration. Kael was carefully bleeding the very last reserves of our chemical thrusters, using short, calculated bursts to slowly nudge the crippled *Zenith* through the debris field. Outside the viewport, the fighters danced through the dark, carving a path for our escape. 

Finally, the shuddering stopped. The massive, city-sized rocks that had threatened to crush us gave way to a vast, open clearing within the asteroid belt. The alien sun hung in the distance, casting long, lonely shadows across our scarred hull. 

We were out of immediate danger, but we were a long way from home.
